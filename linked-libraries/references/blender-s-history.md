# Blender S History

> Blender Linked Libraries and Append/Link reference. Original prose; identifiers preserved verbatim.

The Beginning — Blender came from Ton Roosendaal, a Dutch art director and self-taught developer. Drawn to all things technical and creative, he started an Industrial Design degree but dropped out to launch his own 3D animation studio, NeoGeo, in 1989 (the game console of the same name turned up a year later). First run from Roosendaal's attic, NeoGeo grew fast, winning awards and becoming the largest studio of its kind in the Netherlands.

Roosendaal wrote the first source files named "Blender" on 2 January 1994, still treated as Blender's official birthday. Blender began as an in-house NeoGeo application, grown out of a set of pre-existing tools that included a ray-tracer built for the Amiga. This early version set out to ease a perennial creative frustration: when a fussy client demands change after change, how do you make those changes painlessly? Through a highly configurable approach, Blender aimed to answer that. (Aside: the name nods to a song by the Swiss electronic band Yello.)

Roosendaal sank his savings into a Silicon Graphics workstation. Costing the equivalent of thirty thousand US dollars, that machine led to Blender 1.0, launched in January 1995, whose first proper iteration carried then-novel ideas like a single window the user could subdivide at will.

3D was reckoned commercially dull at the time, but Roosendaal had fallen for what he calls its "magical ability to create a whole world in a computer." So after NeoGeo shut down, he and his partner Frank van Beek set up a new company to keep developing and marketing Blender. Not a Number (NaN) opened in June 1998 and distributed Blender on a freemium model: free to download, with NaN selling keys that unlocked more advanced features.

Blender Goes Open Source — that model let NaN fund a booth at SIGGRAPH (Special Interest Group on Computer Graphics and Interactive Techniques), the renowned Los Angeles computer-graphics conference, and Blender pulled in two funding rounds totaling some five and a half million US dollars. Even so, a harsh economy, overspending, and strained relations between NaN and its investors shut the company down in early 2002.

With NaN gone, Blender's development halted. Unable to buy the rights from NaN's backers, Roosendaal tried something novel: in May 2002 he started a non-profit, the Blender Foundation, aiming to make Blender open-source — a public monument to Blender, and a way for everyone who'd worked on it to keep it for their portfolios. That July he launched the first-ever crowdfunding campaign, Free Blender, and thanks to Blender's 250,000-strong user community the Foundation raised one hundred and ten thousand euros in just seven weeks — enough to reclaim Blender from its investors.

On Sunday, 13 October 2002, Blender was released under the GNU General Public License, the strictest open-source contract there is. Not only would Blender be free, its source would stay free forever, usable for any purpose at all.

Free Blender's success opened the door to a development style that's become Blender's defining strength. Although grant-funded developers partly drive its evolution, guided by a core team at the Blender Foundation in Amsterdam, its biggest edge is a global community of dedicated volunteers; thanks to them, Blender can iterate fast and answer the needs of artists and makers — nimbleness and creativity that would be far harder inside a traditional business model.

Blender Makes Open Movies — to stress-test Blender's growing power, the Foundation challenged its most talented community artists to make an animated 3D short, on the sole condition that they use open-source tools, Blender chief among them. Codenamed "Project Orange," it began in 2005 and produced Elephants Dream, a surreal trip through a giant machine; the film and all its assets were released freely under a Creative Commons license. After that success, the Blender Institute was founded in summer 2007: besides helping shape the Foundation's goals, it set up a permanent office and studio expressly to generate Open Projects around 3D film, games, and visual effects, and it has made a string of Open Movies with leading artists, including the comedy Big Buck Bunny (2008), the sci-fi thriller Tears of Steel (2012), the poetic fantasy Spring (2019), and the horror-comedy Sprite Fright (2021).

Blender Landmarks — each Open Project puts fresh demands on Blender as a 3D suite, which in turn drives more upgrades. A full update list is beyond this article, but a few milestones stand out. Early 2008 kicked off the Blender 2.5 project, joining a big UI overhaul to fresh tool definitions, a data-access layer, event handling, and an all-new animation system; for 2.5 the main goal was to modernize the interface standards and input methods. Cycles, Blender's production-capable path-tracing render engine, first arrived in release 2.61 back in 2011 and has since grown to support a wide range of rendering — AMD and NVIDIA among them — plus support for hair, for motion blur, for smoke and fire, for the big shaders and materials, for adaptive subdivisions, and plenty besides. Its watershed 2.8 release, in July 2019, pushed Blender squarely into the 3D mainstream: starting from a radically revamped UI, the 2.8 series packed in innovation after innovation — EEVEE (a real-time render engine), new remeshing tools for sculptors, the Mantaflow integration, and a fully working 2D animation workspace that also opened up a 2D/3D hybrid workflow.

Industry recognition for Blender had been building for decades, but 2.8 was the moment it was widely accepted as a real alternative to paid rivals. Besides using Blender on their own projects, some of the world's largest and best-known companies became regular contributors to the Blender Development Fund, helping keep Blender innovating.

Alongside Blender and the Open Projects made with it, there's Blender Cloud, a subscription-based Open Production platform that offers rolling updates on current Open Movie projects plus an archive of film assets in .blend form, animation and shot breakdowns, shaders and textures, and thorough training videos from professional artists and developers, many of them based at Blender HQ in Amsterdam.

Blender: Present And Future — all told, the Blender organization runs to some twenty-eight employees working from Amsterdam, remotely, and on grants. For Blender, that team is only a sliver of a far wider community, defined as everyone who contributes to Blender's development, earns a living from it, or simply downloads it. The Blender mission, in short, is to put the world's best 3D technology into the hands of artists as open-source — and to make amazing things with it. Looking ahead, Blender hopes to become a sustainable, future-proof organization committed to its open-source philosophy, its values of curiosity and innovation, technical excellence, and ever more ambitious creative goals.

Version/Revision Milestones — the start!
- 1.00 – January 1994: Blender in development at the NeoGeo animation studio.
- 1.23 – January 1998: SGI version published on the web, IrisGL.
- 1.30 – April 1998: Linux and FreeBSD version, ported to OpenGL and X11.
- 1.3x – June 1998: NaN founded.
- 1.4x – September 1998: Sun and Linux Alpha version released.
- 1.50 – November 1998: first Manual published.
- 1.60 – April 1999: C-key (new features behind a lock, $95), Windows version released.
- 1.6x – June 1999: BeOS and PPC version released.
- 1.80 – June 2000: end of the C-key; Blender full freeware again.
- 2.00 – August 2000: interactive 3D and real-time engine.
- 2.10 – December 2000: new engine, physics, and Python.
- 2.20 – August 2001: character animation system.
- 2.21 – October 2001: Blender Publisher launch.
- 2.2x – December 2001: macOS version.
Blender goes Open Source:
- 13 October 2002: Blender goes Open Source; 1st Blender Conference.
- 2.25 – October 2002: Blender Publisher becomes freely available, and the experimental tree of Blender is created, a coder's playground.
- 2.26 – February 2003: the first truly open-source Blender release.
- 2.27 – May 2003: the second open-source Blender release.
- 2.28x – July 2003: first of the 2.28x series.
- 2.30 – October 2003: preview release of the 2.3x UI makeover shown at the 2nd Blender Conference.
- 2.31 – December 2003: upgrade to the stable 2.3x UI project.
- 2.32 – January 2004: a major overhaul of internal rendering.
- 2.33 – April 2004: Game Engine returns, ambient occlusion, new procedural textures.
- 2.34 – August 2004: particle interactions, LSCM UV mapping, working YafRay integration, weighted creases in subdivision surfaces, ramp shaders, full OSA, and many more.
- 2.35 – November 2004: more improvements — object hooks, curve deforms and tapers, particle duplicators, and much more.
- 2.36 – December 2004: a stabilization version, much behind-the-scenes work, better normal and displacement mapping.
- 2.37 – June 2005: transform tools and widgets, soft bodies, force fields, deflections, incremental subdivision surfaces, transparent shadows, and multi-threaded rendering.
- 2.40 – December 2005: full rework of the armature system, shape keys, fur via particles, fluids, and rigid bodies.
- 2.41 – January 2006: lots of fixes, plus some Game Engine features.
- 2.42 – July 2006: the nodes release — Array modifier, vector blur, a new physics engine, rendering, lip sync, and many more; this followed Project Orange.
- 2.43 – February 2007: multiresolution meshes, multi-layer UV textures, multi-layer images and multi-pass rendering and baking, sculpting, retopology, more mattes, distort and filter nodes, modeling and animation gains, better multi-brush painting, fluid particles, proxy objects, a Sequencer rewrite, and post-production UV texturing.
- 2.44 – May 2007: the big news, besides two new modifiers and reviving 64-bit OS support, was subsurface scattering, which models how light scatters under the surface of soft, organic objects.
- 2.45 – September 2007: serious bug fixes, with some performance issues handled.
- 2.46 – May 2008: the Peach release, the work of over 70 developers, bringing hair and fur, a new particle system, better image browsing, cloth, a seamless non-intrusive physics cache, rendering gains for reflections, AO, and render baking, a Mesh Deform modifier for muscles and the like, improved animation through armature tools and drawing, skinning, constraints, and a colorful Action Editor, and much more; it held the results of Project Peach.
- 2.47 – August 2008: bugfix release.
- 2.48 – October 2008: the Apricot release — cool GLSL shaders, light and GE gains, snap, sky simulator, Shrinkwrap modifier, and Python editing gains; it held the results of Project Apricot.
- 2.49 – June 2009: node-based textures, armature sketching (Etch-a-Ton), Boolean mesh improvements, JPEG2000 support, projection painting for direct image-to-model transfer, and a sizable Python script catalog; GE gains included video textures (play movies in-game), Bullet physics upgrades, dome (fisheye) rendering, and more API GE calls.
Blender 2.5x – The Recode!:
- 2.5x – From 2009 to August 2011: four pre-versions (Alpha 0 in November 2009 through Beta in July 2010) and three stable versions (2.57 in April 2011 to 2.59 in August 2011). One of the most important development projects, with a total software refactor — new functions, a redesigned internal window manager and event/tool/data handling system, and a new Python API; the project's final version was Blender 2.59 in August 2011.
Blender 2.6x to 2.7x – Improvements & Stabilizing.

- 2.60 – October 2011: UI internationalization, gains in the animation system and the GE, vertex weight group modifiers, 3D audio and video, and bug fixes.
- 2.61 – December 2011: the Cycles renderer landed in trunk; a camera tracker; dynamic paint for altering textures via mesh contact/proximity; the Ocean modifier for ocean and foam; new add-ons; bug fixes; and more Python API extensions.
- 2.62 – February 2012: the Carve library for better Boolean operations, object-tracking support, the Remesh modifier, many GE gains, improved matrices and vectors in the Python API, new add-ons, and many bug fixes.
- 2.63 – April 2012: Bmesh merged into trunk with full n-sided polygon support, sculpt hiding, a panoramic Cycles camera, mirror-ball environment textures and float-precision textures, render-layer mask layers, ambient occlusion, and viewport display of background images and render layers; new import/export add-ons; and 150 bug fixes.
- 2.64 – October 2012: a mask editor, an improved motion tracker, OpenColorIO, Cycles gains, Sequencer gains, better mesh tools (Inset and Bevel improved), new keying nodes, sculpt masking, COLLADA gains, a new Skin modifier, a new compositing-nodes backend, and many bug fixes.
- 2.65 – December 2012: fire and smoke gains, an anisotropic Cycles shader, modifier gains, rounding added to the Bevel tool, new add-ons, and 200+ bug fixes.
- 2.66 – February 2013: dynamic topology, rigid-body simulation, UI and usability gains (retina-display support included), Cycles hair support, per-vertex beveling in the Bevel tool, the new Mesh Cache and UV Warp modifiers, and a new SPH particle fluid solver; 250+ bug fixes.
- 2.67 – May 2013: Freestyle, paint-system gains, Cycles subsurface scattering, the Ceres library in the motion tracker, custom Python nodes, new mesh-modeling tools, better UTF-8 text support and Text-editor gains, 3D-printing add-ons, and 260+ bug fixes.
- 2.68 – July 2013: new and improved modeling tools, three new Cycles nodes, big motion-tracker gains, Python scripts and drivers disabled by default on file load for security, and 280+ bug fixes.
- 2.69 – October 2013: still more modeling tools, Cycles gains across many areas, plane tracking in the motion tracker, better FBX import/export, and 270+ bug fixes.
- 2.70 – March 2014: basic CPU volumetrics in Cycles, more motion-tracker gains, two new modeling modifiers, some UI-consistency gains, and 560+ bug fixes.
- 2.71 – June 2014: deformation motion blur and fire/smoke in Cycles, draggable UI pop-ups, sculpt-mode performance work, new animation interpolation types, many GE gains, and 400+ bug fixes.
- 2.72 – October 2014: GPU volume and SSS in Cycles, pie menus, much-improved tooltips, the Intersection modeling tool, a new Sun Beam Compositor node, Freestyle working with Cycles, a better texture-painting workflow, and 220+ bug fixes.
- 2.73 – January 2015: better Cycles volumetrics, a major Grease Pencil upgrade, Input Method Editors (IMEs) on Windows, and general gains to painting, Freestyle, the Sequencer, and add-ons.
- 2.74 – March 2015: custom-normal support, viewport compositing, and hair-dynamics gains.
- 2.75 – July 2015: an integrated stereo/multi-view pipeline, the Smooth Corrective modifier, and a new developmental dependency graph.
- 2.76 – November 2015: Pixar OpenSubdiv support, a Viewport and File Browser speed boost, node auto-offset, and a Sequencer text-effect strip.
- 2.77 – March 2016: OpenVDB caching for smoke/volumetric sims, better Cycles subsurface scattering, Grease Pencil stroke sculpting and workflow gains, and reworked library handling for missing/deleted data-blocks.
- 2.78 – September 2016: Cycles spherical-stereo images for VR, Grease Pencil behaving more like other 2D drawing software, Alembic import/export, and Bendy Bones gains for simpler rigging.
- 2.79 – September 2017: new Cycles features (Denoising, Shadow Catcher, the new Principled shader), more Grease Pencil and Alembic gains, and application-template support.

Blender 2.8 – Revamped UI
- 2.80 – July 2019: a fully redesigned UI for easier navigation; an improved viewport, gizmos, and tools; EEVEE, a new physically based real-time render engine; a big Grease Pencil overhaul, now a full 2D drawing and animation system; collections (replacing the old layers) as a powerful way to organize objects; plus gains to Cycles, Modeling, Animation, Import/Export, and the Dependency Graph.
- 2.81 – November 2019: revamped sculpting tools, Cycles OptiX-accelerated rendering, denoising, many EEVEE gains, library overrides, UI gains, and much more.
- 2.82 – February 2020: UDIM and USD support, Mantaflow for fluid and smoke simulation, AI denoising, Grease Pencil gains, and much more.
- 2.83 LTS – June 2020: 3D-Viewport VR scene inspection, a new Volume object type, Cycles adaptive sampling, Cycles viewport denoising, sculpting gains, and much more; the first LTS release, meant for studios and long-lifecycle projects.

Blender 2.9 – Refining 2.8
- 2.90 – August 2020: an improved sky texture, EEVEE motion blur, sculpting gains, a revamped modifier UI, better modeling tools, and faster Cycles motion blur.
- 2.91 – November 2020: Outliner gains, property search, better mesh Boolean operations, animation curves, Volume-object and display gains, and more refined sculpting tools.
- 2.92 – February 2021: geometry nodes, the primitive Add tool, sculpting gains, Grease Pencil curve editing, Cycles Color Attribute baking, APIC fluid sims, Video Sequencer gains, and much more.
- 2.93 LTS – June 2021: new geometry nodes, sculpting gains, the Grease Pencil Line Art modifier and other gains, an improved EEVEE DOF, a redesigned Cryptomatte workflow, and more; the LTS release for the 2.9 series.

Blender 3.0 – Optimizing Performance
- 3.0 – December 2021: the Asset Browser, Cycles X, EEVEE Attributes, new geometry nodes, an animation update, Grease Pencil Line Art gains, a pose library, Open Image Denoising 2–8× faster, and added AMD-on-Linux support.

- 3.1 – March 2022: major point-cloud gains, Cycles Apple Metal GPU support, GPU subdivision support, larger-image handling in the image editor, big geometry-nodes performance gains, and context-aware geometry-node search.
- 3.2 – June 2022: Cycles light groups, true shadow caustics, volume motion blur, glTF gains, AMD GPU rendering on Linux, painting in sculpt mode, and WebP image support.
- 3.3 LTS – September 2022: a new Hair object, procedural UV nodes, Line Art shadow and contour, Intel GPU rendering via oneAPI, and library-override gains; the first LTS of the 3.0 series.
- 3.4 – December 2022: Cycles path guiding, sculpting auto-masking gains, still more geometry nodes, UV-editing gains, and Wayland support on Linux.
- 3.5 – March 2023: new generative hair assets, vector displacement maps for sculpting, the viewport compositor, and Cycles light trees.
- 3.6 LTS – June 2023: Simulation Nodes added to Geometry Nodes, Cycles hardware ray-tracing for AMD and Intel, UV island packing, a bundled asset pack from Blender Studio and community artists, and a new retopology overlay; the final LTS of the 3.0 series.

Blender 4.0 – A Major Leap For Rendering, Creating Tools, and More
- 4.0 – November 2023: a new Principled BSDF shader with coat and sheen layers, the AgX view transform, Voronoi Texture fractal noise, light linking for selective lighting, running Geometry Nodes as Node Tools, snapping gains including Snap Base, type-to-search for menus and modifier types, the new Inter typeface, a streamlined keymap, bone collections, the Hydra Storm USD renderer, a larger asset library, and alignment to the VFX Reference Platform 2023.
- 4.1 – March 2024: Geometry Nodes baking, the Menu Switch node, OpenImageDenoise GPU acceleration, more real-time viewport-compositor functions, simpler animation-keyframe insertion, hierarchical bone collections, graph-editor click-and-slide, Video Sequencer performance and color-scope gains, alignment to the VFX Reference Platform 2024, and armature and shape-key export to USD.
- 4.2 LTS – July 2024: the next generation of EEVEE with major upgrades to lighting, sun lights, displacement, subsurface, volumetrics, and motion blur; Cycles gains a Ray Portal BSDF and Thin-Film Interference, plus better low-noise soft-volume rendering and blue-noise sampling; the Blender Extensions platform launches; the Khronos PBR Neutral Tone Mapper; sculpting-selection gains; matrix inputs for nodes; Node Tools that can use mouse position and viewport; a Video Sequencer graphical overhaul; more USD export options; and native portable-installation support. The first LTS of the 4.0 series.
- 4.3 – November 2024: light linking and shadow linking in EEVEE, the Metallic BSDF, the Gabor noise texture, EEVEE render passes in the compositor, minimum-stretch (SLIM) UV unwrapping, many Geometry Nodes updates including the For-Each zone, physics nodes, a Grease Pencil engine rewritten for speed and features, 100+ default brushes for painting and sculpting, and UI area docking.
