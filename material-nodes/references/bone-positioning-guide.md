# Bone Positioning Guide, Generated Rig Features, Skin

## Overview

Covers: Bone Positioning Guide; Generated Rig Features; Skin.

## Bone Positioning Guide

Rigify bone-positioning guide. Face: begin by reading the basic face landmarks that guide placement — in the reference imagery, orange marks bones that sit in closed loops, yellow marks bones whose position depends on surrounding loops, red marks outer-edge bones, and purple marks bridging bones that cover deforming flesh. The eyes-nose loop is divided into named parts; aligning the mid bones across "brow", "brow.b", "lid.t" and the cheek improves results after generation. The jaw-ear area is likewise split into named parts; place "ear.L" over the part of the ear attached to the lower jaw, and use the temple bone to cover what you don't want moving with the jaw, which also fixes the jaw pivot. Always preserve merge points — wherever bone heads or tails overlap at one point, keep them overlapping after moving, since tearing a merge point apart can spawn duplicate controls or generation errors. After the main face bones, use the cheek bone to tie the eye-nose area to the jaw-mouth area and the brow area similarly, which auto-defines the face's compression areas. Place the eye bones at the eye pivot facing forward along Y, with length matching the eye radius (for a spherical eye, find the pivot in Edit Mode by selecting two opposite vertices on the center meridian and snapping the cursor to the selection; a full, unapplied sphere can just use its center of mass). Finally set the teeth bones on the teeth and the tongue chain per the figure (the tongue works best with bones on the symmetry line), and confirm the face master bone faces upward before generating. Torso: identify the rigid zones (head, chest, pelvis) which need fewer bones — good edge-loop placement at zone boundaries aids deformation — then, from side view, lay the main spine using one bone for rigid areas and two for flexible ones, plus extra pelvis bones (to counter leg bend), two breast controls, and two shoulder bones; the pelvis bones won't be final controls but still drive deformation. Keep the spine roughly centered in the mesh with a slight backward offset, and treat shoulder bones as general deformers (placing them too far forward, at the collarbone, can deform badly). Limbs: for arms, start with a straight shoulder-to-hand line in front and top views, then add a slight elbow bend by sliding the forearm/upper-arm junction toward world Y in top view; do the leg the same way (hip-to-ankle straight line, then place foot and toe, with a slight knee bend by sliding the thigh/shin junction toward world Y in side view), and align the heel bone in front view to span the foot, then in side view to where the heel meets the floor. Since version 0.5, manual bone-roll alignment is no longer needed — the generate function derives it from the bend axis, so just add a slight bend (for more orientation control, see Advanced Usage). Fingers: place each knuckle (an easy method is to snap the 3D cursor to the corresponding mesh edge loop in Edit Mode, then snap the bone to it), then fix bone rolls with X as the bend axis — finger axes can be made consistent by selecting all finger bones and using Recalculate Roll ▸ Global -Z Axis.

The thumb often needs extra tweaking by topology; Recalculate Roll ▸ Global +Y Axis is a good starting point. Once rolls are consistent, generate the rig and scale the finger master controls — the fingers should curl, and if they rotate on the wrong axis, change the Bend Rotation Axis on the first finger's parameters under Rigify Type. With the fingers placed, position the palm bones: keep the palm bones' heads slightly apart (Rigify needs that gap to build the palm control hierarchy), and align the palm axes by selecting all palm bones and using Recalculate Roll ▸ Global -Z Axis. See Bone Structure and Bone Roll for more on bones and rolls.

## Generated Rig Features

Generated rig features. After human generation, a new armature named "rig" appears, holding all the features. Common features — Rig UI Panels: the rig comes with a script that adds panels to the Item tab of the 3D-view sidebar when one of its bones is active. Rig Bake Settings (shown when the armature has an active Action) serves operators that act on many keyframes: Bake All Keyed Frames keys the result on every frame any bone is keyed (not just relevant ones); Limit Frame Range confines the operator to a range; Start/End set that range; Get Frame Range copies it from the scene. Rig Main Properties shows properties and operators relevant to the selected bones. Rig Layers holds buttons for toggling bone-collection visibility, with layout and labels defined by the metarig's Bone Collection UI panel. Shared controls: every Rigify rig is assembled from standardized sub-rigs wired into a parent-child hierarchy, and although each sub-rig behaves in its own way, many follow common conventions. Root bone: every rig has a "root" bone parenting all others, assigned to a Root collection, placed at the rig object's origin unless the metarig supplies a custom one, with a circle-plus-four-arrows widget. Limb Master: at the base of many limb-style sub-rigs sits a gear-shaped bone that can, in some cases, shift the entire sub-rig as one rigid unit and also holds the custom properties shown in Rig Main Properties (so it's usually the bone to inspect in the Graph editor); when several controls each need their own copy of conceptually one property, it may instead sit on those controls. Tweak controls: blue spheres in the default scheme, the final control layer above the deform bones; they follow the general IK/FK position but can be moved, twisted, and scaled freely into near-impossible shapes. Rubber Tweak: some sub-rigs add a slider in Rig Main Properties when tweaks are selected, controlling the Bendy Bone joint's smoothness at that point — 0 gives a sharp bend, 1 a smooth, rubber-hose look. Custom pivots: freely movable bones (like IK controls) can carry an optional custom pivot control (an axes empty capped with squares or crosses); move it to relocate the pivot, then rotate or scale to transform the target around it. IK and FK switching: many rig types offer both IK (red) and FK (green) controls with switching and snapping — a Rig Main Properties slider blends full IK at 0 to full FK at 1, while snapping one set onto the other uses a trio of buttons: the main button snaps on the current frame (auto-keying if enabled), the Action button bakes the change over multiple keyframes per Rig Bake Settings, and the Clear button deletes keyframes on those controls within the bake interval. Parent switching: some freely movable controls (often IK) can switch their parent among choices including the root or none, exposed in Rig Main Properties as a row of three — a dropdown-menu button that switches parent on the current frame while preserving world position/orientation, a dropdown field exposing the switch property for keyframing and direct manipulation (changing it can make the bone jump), and a button to apply the position-preserving switch over the bake range; when you manually add a Child Of constraint to the control, set the built-in parent to none. Limbs have a master bone and tweaks, with multiple deform segments and tweaks per the meta-rig options, and the IK control may carry an optional custom pivot plus predefined pivots. The sidebar shows: FK Limb Follow (at 1 the FK limb keeps its rotation relative to the root instead of following the torso), IK-FK (blends full IK at 0 to full FK at 1), and IK↔FK snapping buttons.

Limb controls continued. IK Stretch blends between the limb stretching freely (1) and being capped at its maximum length (0). Toggle Pole Switch: off, the IK limb uses the rotational pole vector (the arrow at the limb base), which you rotate/translate/scale to drive the IK base; on, the classic pole vector appears and orients the limb while the arrow still handles the base's scale and location — and, like Parent Switching, the row offers buttons that either convert the current pose from one type to the other or bake that change across the whole action. IK Parent Switch reparents the main IK control; Pole Parent Switch reparents the classic IK pole control. Arms have the simplest structure — the IK controls are the main IK control, the optional custom pivot, and the optional wrist control (a bent circle) that pivots around the hand bone's tail rather than its head — with nothing extra in Rig Main Properties. Legs are more involved: optional separate IK and FK toe controls (on by default in the bundled metarigs and recommended for stable IK↔FK snapping); an IK Heel control rotated to roll forward/backward, rock sideways, or yaw the heel; an optional Toe Pivot rotating around the toe base; and an optional custom pivot. Their properties panel adds two features — IK→FK Snap With Roll buttons (ordinary IK→FK snapping resets the other IK controls, folding roll and rock into the main control, which is awkward when animating with the heel; this alternative deduces the heel rotation to keep the main IK control parallel to the ground inferred from its current orientation, with options for which heel axes to use), and an optional Roll On Toe slider (controls whether heel rotation, minus backward roll, applies at the toe base or tip). Fingers and tentacles — Simple Tentacle: the simplest finger/appendage rig, with only basic FK controls and tweaks, its sole automation being the option to copy certain local-rotation axes of one FK control to the next. Advanced Finger: a dedicated finger sub-rig offering a Master control (orange) to rotate the whole finger and bend it via Y scaling, an FK chain (green) that can also translate as semi-tweaks, and an optional IK control for the tip (red) — IK here is rudimentary, meant as an FK adjustment (pose in FK, then enable IK after an IK→FK snap to pin the tip); its panel has an optional Finger IK slider, optional FK↔IK snapping buttons (snap the IK control to the finger end, or fit the FK controls to the IK result), and a Curvature slider (the same rubber-hose effect as Rubber Tweak). Spline Tentacle: an advanced flexible-appendage rig built on the Spline IK constraint, where IK control bones drive a Bezier spline's control points that the IK chain follows. It generates in three modes: Stretch To Fit (every deform-chain bone follows the curve and squashes/stretches to its length), Manual Squash & Stretch (the chain doesn't auto-scale to the curve length; it covers as much as its manually scaled length allows, overhanging and straightening — possibly with jitter — if the curve is too short), and Direct Tip Control (more like IK limbs: the tip IK control directly drives the chain's last bone while the others stretch along the curve to bridge the gap). Its control bones: Master (the same gear master as other limbs), IK Start (at the base; controls base twist and sideways scale, a switchable parent for other IK controls, and in Manual Squash & Stretch the tentacle's uniform scale), optional IK Start Extra controls (hidden by default, parented to IK Start, optionally affecting chain thickness via curve-point radius), and IK Middle (mid-curve controls parented to Master by default but settable to IK Start or IK End, also optionally affecting thickness).

Spline tentacle, continued. Optional IK End Extra controls are hidden by default, parented to IK End, and can affect chain thickness via curve-point radius; the Direct Tip Control mode adds one more end control beside the middle ones that can't be hidden. IK End drives the curve's last control point and is a potential parent for the other chain controls, and in Direct Tip Control it also directly drives the chain's last bone. The optional IK End Twist control is visually attached to the chain's last bone and must use Euler rotation: in Stretch To Fit it twists the tentacle tip (fading to nothing at the base), in Manual Squash & Stretch it also scales the tip, and in Direct Tip Control it doesn't exist. An optional FK Chain adds a fully FK control chain. Its properties: an optional Start/End Controls count (how many extra controls are visible/active — a disabled one snaps very close to its end control point to neutralize it, so changing this mid-animation can jump, and the plus/minus buttons help keep continuity by keyframing the count with Constant interpolation while snapping and keying the control to its hidden spot); an optional End Twist Estimate (in Direct Tip Control the end twist is read from the free tip orientation rather than a constrained Euler control, which only resolves within 180° of neutral, so for long tentacles this sets an approximate twist, shifting the neutral point so the automatic correction works within 180° of it); optional IK-FK / IK↔FK snapping (when FK controls exist — but here automatic IK-to-FK snapping is only approximate and needs manual tuning, so no bake-over-range buttons are offered); and Parent Switch (reparents the selected IK control). Spine, head, and tail — Spine: a cube-shaped torso control with switchable parent, plus subordinate bent-circle hip and chest controls and low-level tweaks; the torso control can take an optional custom pivot, and the rig can optionally add a full FK control set beneath the simplified ones but above tweaks, with the spine's properties panel usually also covering the head and/or tail. Head: attaches at the spine's end, giving rotational head and neck controls plus neck tweaks; with a neck three or more bones long, an extra translational neck-bend control appears (a circle-with-arrows widget). Its options: Neck Follow (isolates neck rotation — neck follows the Torso at 0, the Chest at 1) and Head Follow (same for the head — Torso at 0, Chest at 1). Tail: attaches at the spine's start, giving FK tail controls plus a master that copies its local rotation around certain axes to every tail bone; its option is Tail Follow (tail follows the Torso at 0, the Hips at 1). Face (the new-style modular face from the Upgrade Face button): its foundation is a network of Bendy Bone chains with a control at every bone end that affects all bones meeting there — translating a control keeps the B-Bone chain's normal automatic bezier-handle behavior, with local rotation/scaling layered on top; for some chains the end and/or middle controls' transforms interpolate to controls between them (often shown with different colors/shapes), and some controls carry constraints that partially copy transform from nearby points. Specialized controllers: areas like the eyes or mouth add controllers with custom behavior on top of the chains. Eyes (besides the eyelid chains): Master (a large circle to move the whole eye), Common Target (a large control enveloping the eye targets, with switchable parent, setting where the eyes look), and Eye Target (small circles inside the common target, one per eye, whose local scale can also drive the iris or pupil depending on weighting).

Eye properties: Eyelids Follow controls how much the eyeball's rotation moves the eyelids (it can split into separate horizontal and vertical sliders depending on the generation options); an optional Eyelids Attached slider can disable the mechanism that forces the lids to conform to the eye's sphere; and a Parent switch selects the common target's parent. Mouth controls: Jaw Master rotates the jaw, driving the main jaw deform bone and the chains belonging entirely to the jaw, while the lip-loop chains adjust to open the mouth as the jaw moves; Mouth Master uniformly transforms the lips without moving the jaw. Its option is a Mouth Lock slider, raised from 0 to 1 to suppress the mouth opening when the jaw rotates or moves.

## Skin

Rigify skin rigs implement a flexible skin system from multiple interacting B-Bone chains, built as the foundation of the new modular Rigify face rig. Its main ideas: a generic B-Bone chain (most deformation should come from one powerful, standard B-Bone chain rig whose chains gain advanced behavior by interacting with other components, rather than many domain-specific rigs each generating their own deform chains — there are two versions, skin.basic_chain, which only attaches B-Bones to the controls with no automation on the controls themselves, and skin.stretchy_chain, which additionally interpolates the motion of the end, and an optional middle, control to the chain's other controls); automatic control merging (the deformation part is chains of one or more B-Bones connecting control points (nodes), and whenever two chains' controls fully overlap they merge automatically, with one chain chosen as owner by heuristics — parent depth from root, the presence of .T/.B/.L/.R symmetry markers, and finally alphabetical order — overridable by an explicit priority where it guesses wrong; the owner and its parents set the extra automation placed on the control, and when a control merges with its .T/.B/.L/.R counterparts, detected purely by naming, the automation of all the owner's symmetry siblings is averaged); parent controllers (instead of just using the parent meta-rig bone (ORG) as the controls' parent, the system provides an interface for parent rigs that supply parent bones and generate control-parent automation for their child chain controls by inheriting from the right base and overriding methods, so a rig can manage its child chains intelligently and add area-specific automation — the base system includes a simple example, skin.transform.basic, which translates its child control points by its control bone's transform); and custom rigging (two extra components integrate custom automation from the meta-rig — skin.anchor generates one control with inherited constraints like basic.super_copy but also joins the skin system as a zero-length, highest-priority chain so a user-controlled point can override normal behavior and have other chains attach to it; and skin.glue attaches itself to the control generated at its position (an error if none exists), reading that control's position from custom meta-rig rigging or injecting constraints into it, and it can also detect the control at the glue bone's tail and use it as a constraint target to copy transform between controls). skin.basic_chain bridges controls with B-Bones and adds no automation to the controls: merely moving them behaves like standard automatic handles, while rotating and optionally scaling them adjusts the result. Its options: B-Bone Segments (set to 1 disables all advanced behavior and just spans the points with a single Stretch To bone); Merge Parent Rotation and Scale (lets the chain respond to rotation/scale from the parents of controls owned by other chains this one merged into); Use Handle Scale (let control scale drive the B-Bone's scale and/or easing); Connect With Mirror (smoothly connect the chain ends when merging controls with the .T/.B/.L/.R counterpart — enable on both chains); Connect Matching Ends (connect the chain's end to the opposite end of a different chain when merging, forming one continuous chain in the same direction — enable on both); Sharpen Corner (generate a mechanism to form a sharp corner at the connected end based on the angle of adjacent control locations, easing reducing from 1 to 0 once the angle gets sharper than the set value); Orientation (orient the controls like the chosen bone rather than aligned to the chain); Copy To Selected (copy to selected rigs sharing the option, so you can select bones indiscriminately without assigning needless values); and Chain Priority (override the owner-selection heuristic). skin.stretchy_chain extends the basic chain with automation that propagates the start, end, and optional middle control's motion to the others, so moving an end stretches the whole chain rather than just the adjacent B-Bones. Its options: Middle Control Position (where the middle control sits; disabled at zero); Falloff (the start/middle/end influence-falloff curves — 0 is linear, higher widens the influence, and -10 fully disables that control's propagation); Spherical Falloff (toggles the curve shape from a power curve that at falloff 1 forms the parabola \(1 - x^{2^f}\) to one forming the circle \((1 - x^{2^f})^{2^{-f}}\)); Falloff Along Chain Curve (compute falloff along the chain's length rather than projecting onto the straight start-end line); and Propagate Twist (whether chain twist propagates to control points between the main controls).

skin.stretchy_chain, continued: Propagate Scale (whether perpendicular chain scaling propagates to control points between the main controls); Propagate to Controls (expose this chain's propagated twist and scale to other chains through Merge Parent Rotation and Scale once their controls merge into it, rather than keeping it local); Primary Control Layers (optional bone collections for the end controls); and Secondary Control Layers (optional collections for the middle control, falling back to Primary if unset). The main controls with active falloff get Merge Parent Rotation and Scale enabled automatically, just for them. skin.anchor effectively acts as a zero-length, highest-priority chain, so it becomes the owner when merging controls with other chains and can feed custom automation influence into the skin system; every constraint on the meta-rig bone moves to the created control. Its options: Generate Deform Bone (a deform bone parented to the control); Suppress Control (make the control a hidden mechanism bone); Widget Type (which widget to generate); Relink Constraints (as in basic.raw_copy, but all constraints move from ORG to the control); and Orientation (the bone used to orient the control, as for other chains). skin.glue is conceptually like skin.anchor but instead of overriding controls it reads or adjusts the state of controls generated by other rigs; its bone head must overlap another skin rig's control. It sets up its ORG bone to read the control's state while moving the bone's original constraints to the control. Its options: Glue Mode (how the ORG bone connects to the skin control) — Child Of Control (make ORG a child of the control), Mirror Of Control (make ORG a sibling with a Copy Transforms constraint from the control, so its local-space transform equals the control's), and Mirror With Parents (parent ORG to the parent automation a glue-owned control would have had while following the actual control, folding both the control's direct and parent-induced motion into the bone's local-space transform); Deformation Bridge (beyond adding glue constraints, the rig also acts as a one-segment basic deform chain, handy when a pair of controls needs both glue and a deform bone); Relink Constraints (as in basic.raw_copy, but constraints move from ORG to the control); Use Tail Target (relink TARGET, or any constraint with an empty target bone and no relink spec, to the control at the glue bone's tail); Target Local With Parents (make the tail target behave like Mirror With Parents); and Add Constraint (add a typical glue constraint with a chosen Influence, as if at the start of the ORG bone's constraint stack). skin.transform.basic is a simplistic parent controller that uses ordinary translation, rotation, or scale to change its child chain controls' locations (but not their orientations or scale); its Generate Control option chooses whether to make a visible control or use the ORG bone's transform as part of a more complex setup.
