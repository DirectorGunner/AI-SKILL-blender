## Light Paths

### Light Paths

Reference

Panel :

Render ‣ Light Paths

### Ray Types

Four categories describe rays:

- Camera: rays originating directly from the camera.

- Reflection: rays generated by surface reflection.

- Transmission: rays generated by surface transmission.

- Shadow: rays for (transparent) shadow calculation.

Reflection and transmission rays carry further properties:

- Diffuse: rays from diffuse reflection or transmission (translucency).

- Glossy: rays from glossy specular reflection or transmission.

- Singular: rays from perfectly sharp reflection or transmission.

The Light Path node identifies the ray type being shaded.

See also

Object ray visibility configuration.

### Bounce Control

Manual control adjusts the maximum light bounces. Ideally infinite, practical rendering often uses fewer bounces, or deliberately omits certain interactions to accelerate convergence. Diffuse reflection, glossy reflection, and transmission bounce counts are independently tunable.

Light paths terminate probabilistically when minimum bounces fall below maximum. This way, longer paths likely contributing minimal light randomly halt, maintaining convergence to identical results while accelerating renders with possible noise increase.

### Transparency

Transparent BSDF receives special treatment. Rays pass straight through without changing direction or type, behaving as if no geometry existed.

Alpha pass handling differs for transparent BSDF . Other transmission BSDFs are opaque—they alter light direction and cannot support alpha-over compositing, unlike transparent BSDF.

Despite rays passing through as though no surface was struck, each transparency step executes shading and traces a ray, affecting performance.

### Clamping

Direct Light
Limits peak intensity from rays not yet bounced. Reduces noise, sacrificing precision. Zero disables clamping. Lower values darken samples more than higher values.

Note

This provides Firefly mitigation, but as you exclude high values, other bright light and reflections dim too.

Care when using this setting balances firefly suppression with preserving intentional bright areas. Clamping indirect bounces separately proves useful since they tend to produce more fireflies than direct bounces. See Clamp Indirect setting.

Indirect Light
Same as Direct Light , but for rays after multiple bounces.

### Caustics

Caustics rank among common noise culprits.

See also

See Reducing Noise for clamping examples.

Filter Glossy
Values above 0.0 blur glossy reflections after diffuse bounces, cutting noise at precision cost. 1.0 offers a suitable starting adjustment.

Certain light paths occur rarely while contributing substantially. Such paths appear in some pixels but not others, generating Fireflies. A difficult path occurs when a small light creates a compact specular highlight on a sharp glossy surface, viewed through a rough glossy layer—approximating a caustic.

Path tracing struggles to locate the specular point, yet boosting the roughness enlarges and softens the highlight, easing detection. Often, existing material roughness masks this blurring, but sometimes detail loss occurs.

Caustics

Reflective
In theory, path tracing renders caustics given sufficient samples, yet practically, the noise proves prohibitive. Deactivate this option to suppress reflective caustics.

Refractive
Similar to reflective caustics: practically, noise becomes excessive. Deactivate to exclude refractive caustics.

Light Paths — Direct and Indirect Control

### Clamping

Reference

### Surface

Direct Light
Limits maximum light intensity a surface can reflect. Reduces aliasing and fireflies at accuracy cost. Zero disables clamping. Lower values produce stronger effects.

Indirect Light
Similar to Direct Light but limits ray-traced and light-probe reflected intensity. Also restricts world lighting intensity since it counts as indirect.

Note
These options suppress fireflies and aliasing on reflective surfaces and dense volumes. However, intentionally bright highlights may also reduce. Balance firefly reduction against desired brightness retention.

### Volume

Direct Light
Same as Surface Direct Light applied to volume direct illumination.

Indirect Light
Same as Surface Indirect Light applied to volume indirect illumination.

### Intensity

Reference
Panel: Render ‣ Light Paths ‣ Intensity

Intensity multipliers scale lighting contribution in EEVEE. They do not alter ray depth, sampling, accumulation, or performance traits.

Direct Light
Scale direct lighting contribution, including light sources directly illuminating surfaces and volumes.

Higher values amplify highlights and direct illumination. Lower values weaken apparent light source strength without modifying their energy settings.

Indirect Light
Scale indirect lighting contribution, including bounced light from ray tracing and light probes.

Affects global illumination and world lighting. Strengthen or weaken perceived bounce light in a scene. Scaling occurs post-clamping, maintaining firefly suppression. Behavior resembles post-lighting compositing adjustment while remaining uniform across dithered and blended materials.

These controls adjust lighting balance in expansive environments, fine-tune indirect illumination, and balance direct versus indirect contribution without altering individual light settings.

### Motion Blur

Reference

Panel :

Render ‣ Motion Blur

Standard Blender renders animations as still frames. While appropriate for stop-motion or time-lapse, real-world cameras capture moving subjects as blurred in their trajectory direction in both video and stills.

(blend-file)

Center on Frame :

Shutter is completely open at the current frame.

End on Frame :

Shutter fully closes at the current frame.

Shutter
Duration (in frames) from shutter open to fully closed. Example: 1.0 shutter time blurs across a single frame.

Rolling Shutter
Emulates a Rolling Shutter effect.

None :

No rolling shutter.

Top-Bottom :

Rolling shutter moves from image top to bottom.

Rolling Shutter Duration
Balances pure rolling shutter effect (zero value) against pure motion blur (one value).

Note

If scenes contain particles or physics simulations, bake them prior to rendering for correct and consistent motion blur.

See also

Individual object settings customize motion blur. Find these in the Object tab of the Properties. Consult object configuration for particulars.

### Shutter Curve

Curve controlling shutter opening and closing. X represents time; Y=0 indicates fully closed, Y=1 fully open. The default maps instantaneous open and close.

### Limitations

- Orthographic Cameras do not support camera motion blur.

- Light motion is disregarded in motion blur calculations.

Motion Blur — Temporal Shutter

Reference
Panel: Render ‣ Motion Blur

Blender renders animations as still image sequences by default. While suitable for stop-motion and time-lapses, this diverges from reality; fast motion exhibits directional blur, visible in film frames and photographs.

Note
Viewport motion blur during animation playback uses a simpler algorithm than final render. Viewport Renders use the same algorithm.

Position
Controls when the shutter opens relative to the current frame.

Start on Frame:
Shutter begins opening at the current frame.

Center on Frame:
Shutter is fully open at the current frame.

End on Frame:
Shutter closes at the current frame.

Shutter
Duration (in frames) between shutter open and close.

Bleeding Bias
Used by post-process blur to prevent background blur onto foreground surfaces. Lower values reduce foreground contamination.

Max Blur
Intended as optimization by limiting the pixel distance across which blur is computed.

Steps
Accumulation blur accuracy control; higher counts increase render duration. More steps improve quality.

Note
Multiple time steps round the render sample count up to the next Steps multiple, ensuring even sample distribution. This can lower jitter camera depth of field quality. Increase viewport or render samples to restore quality.

EEVEE splits rendering into multiple time steps and accumulates results, called Accumulation Motion Blur. This approach is precise but requires numerous steps for smooth gradients. Combined with post-process blur for inter-step gaps, each step demands full scene re-evaluation, adding overhead. Increasing steps also allows reducing Max Blur as post-process blur covers less distance.

Shutter Curve
Enable custom shutter curve.

### Example

| No motion blur applied. | Post-process blur only. |
| 4 time steps without post-process blur. | 4 time steps with post-process blur. |
| 32 time steps without post-process blur. | 32 time steps with post-process blur. |

## Sampling

### Sampling

Reference

Panel :

Render ‣ Sampling

Sampling traces rays from the camera into the environment, bouncing until striking a light source—a Light object, emissive mesh, or world backdrop. This ray-tracing method is termed the integrator.

Viewport (Max) Samples
Per-pixel sample count in the 3D Viewport (Rendered shading mode). Zero activates indefinite sampling.

Render (Max) Samples
Per-pixel path count in final rendering. Higher counts yield cleaner results, requiring longer computation.

Time Limit
Halts rendering upon exceeding the time threshold, even if sample targets remain unmet. Zero disables the limit.

Note

Time limit excludes preprocessing—only active render time counts.

### Adaptive Sampling

When Noise Threshold activates, Cycles applies adaptive sampling, curtailing sampling in areas sufficiently clean versus the threshold—avoiding effort on satisfactory regions. Example: character hair may require many samples, yet the background needs few.

Noise Threshold
Quality cutoff for continuing sample investment per pixel. Standard ranges: 0.1 to 0.001, with lower thresholds ensuring superior quality but extended renders. Zero makes Cycles set an automatic threshold from total sample count.

Min Samples
Minimum samples per pixel before adaptive sampling engages. At 0 (default), Cycles picks based on the Noise Threshold .

### Denoising

Denoising employs a dedicated algorithm achieving cleaner results without higher sample counts.

See also

Activating the Denoising Data render pass enables post-render denoising through the Denoise Node in compositing.

Activate denoising in Rendered viewport shading mode.

Denoise (Render)
Activate denoising for final rendering.

Denoiser
Algorithm selection.

Automatic :

Uses GPU acceleration when available for best performance. Favors OpenImageDenoise over OptiX.

OpenImageDenoise :

Intel's Open Image Denoise, an AI-based denoiser. Typically delivers top-tier quality.

NVIDIA's OptiX AI denoiser. Some older NVIDIA GPUs without OpenImageDenoise support benefit. Only accessible with OptiX configuration in Cycles Render Devices preferences.

None :

Color-only denoising.

Albedo :

Color and albedo denoising.

Color, albedo, and normal denoising.

High :

Speed-prioritized (viewport rendering ideal).

### Path Guiding

Path guiding diminishes noise where light discovery proves difficult for regular path tracing—for instance, rooms lit through narrow portals. Important light orientations learn incrementally, refining with additional samples.

Note

- Path guiding requires CPU-only rendering.

- While path guiding aids caustic rendering in some cases, it is not optimized for intricate caustics as they resist guidance.

See also

Use Portals Area Lights for manual path trace direction.

Training Samples
Peak training sample count. Zero sustains training until render completion. Typically 128 to 256 training samples provide sufficient direction. More training slightly improves direction yet prolongs renders.

Surface
Activate surface diffuse and glossy path guiding.

Volume
Activate volume interior path guiding.

### Lights

Light Tree
Activate light tree for refined scene light sampling, evaluating position and intensity. May substantially lower noise at slightly extended per-sample duration.

Certain light properties are absent from the light tree: custom falloff, ray visibility, and intricate shader networks including textures. This can increase noise in scenes using these capabilities.

Light Threshold
Probabilistically skip light sampling when contribution falls below threshold. Avoids squandering samples on negligible-impact lights. Zero disables.

Scrambling distance multiplier. Below one reduces distance, potentially accelerating GPU work yet increasing artifact visibility.

Minimal light bounces per-path, thereafter Russian Roulette probabilistically halts paths contributing minimally. Higher settings yield less noise, potentially lengthening render duration. For low maximum bounces, match minimum bounces.

Min Transparent Bounces
Minimum transparent bounces (passthroughs). Higher values reduce opening-bounce noise but may reduce effectiveness for complex shapes like hair and volumes.

Layer Samples
Controls View Layer Sample Override application.

Permit layer overrides of scene sample count.

Sample Subset
Render a partial sample count. Multiple subset renders combine into totals via Python Console:

bpy.ops.cycles.merge_images(input_filepath1=r"1.exr", input_filepath2=r"2.exr",
output_filepath=r"combined.exr")

Typical use: spread frame rendering over machines. Example: 2048 total samples across two systems:

- Both: set (Max) Samples to 2048, disable Denoise , activate Sample Subset .

- Machine 1: Offset 0, Length 1024.

- Machine 2: Offset 1024, Length 1024.

- Run 1024-sample renders on each, merge results for 2048-sample equivalent.

Offset
Zero-indexed first sample position in subset.

Length
Subset sample count. This overrides (Max) Samples for rendering samples, but (Max) Samples must still match total subset samples—mismatched noise creates inferior merges.
