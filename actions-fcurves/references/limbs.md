# Limbs, Rotation Modes, Extensions Command Line Arguments, Keymap Customization ...

> Blender Actions, F-Curves, and Animation reference. Original prose; identifiers preserved verbatim.

## Overview

Covers: Limbs; Rotation Modes; Extensions Command Line Arguments; Keymap Customization; Bone Roll; Delete; Duplicate; Extrude.

## Limbs

Rigify limb rig types generate the various limbs and their features (including fingers). limbs.simple_tentacle makes a simple bendy, stretchy B-Bone tentacle chain that can optionally copy local rotation from earlier bones to later ones (useful for fingers); it needs a chain of at least two connected bones. Automation Axis (X, Y, Z, or None — Shift-LMB for multiple or none) turns on that copying per axis: later control bones then copy the local rotation of earlier ones, exposed in the final rig as a Copy Rotation constraint that can be disabled after generation or at animation time. Assign Tweak Layers lets the Tweak controls live in different bone collections from the main controls. limbs.super_finger makes a bendy, stretchy finger chain whose master control rotates every joint through its scale; it needs a chain of at least two connected bones. Bend Rotation Axis (Automatic, X, Y, Z, -X, -Y, -Z) sets the automatic rotation axis tied to the master's scale; B-Bone Segments sets how many segments each tweak control splits into; and IK Control adds a very simple one-control IK mechanism. That IK begins from the FK-defined finger shape and adjusts it to make the fingertip reach the IK control — it's meant to pin a fingertip to a surface temporarily, not as a full posing system; to keep it fast, the IK control's switchable parent offers only one option besides None, so it's advised to add a "held object" control (using basic.raw_copy) as the fingers' common parent with a full parent switch. IK Local Location sets the IK controls' Local Location option, deciding whether the location channels align to the local control orientation or to world. Assign Tweak Layers and Assign Extra IK Layers place the tweak controls and the extra IK control, respectively, in their own bone collections. Note that the rotation axis (Bend Rotation Axis for limbs.super_finger) affects the generated bones' roll — Automatic recalculates it, while any Manual mode copies the meta-rig bones' roll. limbs.super_limb is a backwards-compatibility wrapper around limbs.arm, limbs.leg, and limbs.paw. limbs.arm makes a fully featured bendy, stretchy arm per the chosen options; it needs three connected bones (upper_arm, forearm, hand). Its options: IK Wrist Pivot (an extra control parented under the hand IK control, pivoting around the hand bone's tail); Rotation Axis (Automatic, X, Z — the IK chain's bend axis, while FK chains rotate freely on all axes); Limb Segments (how many extra tweak controls each limb bone gets); B-Bone Segments; Custom IK Pivot (an extra end-of-IK control to rotate around an arbitrarily placed pivot); and Assign FK Layers / Assign Tweak Layers (place those controls in their own collections, separate from the IK bones). limbs.leg makes a fully featured bendy, stretchy leg per the options; it needs four connected bones (thigh, shin, foot, toe) plus one unconnected child of the foot used as the heel pivot. Its options: Foot Pivot (Ankle, Toe, or Ankle & Toe — where the main IK control's pivot sits, or whether to add a pivot control at the toe base); Separate IK Toe (two distinct IK and FK toe controls rather than one shared bone, needed for fully correct IK-FK snapping in every pose); Toe Tip Roll (a slider switching the heel control to pivot on the toe tip rather than its base — for roll, only on forward roll); plus Rotation Axis, Limb Segments, B-Bone Segments, Custom IK Pivot, and the Assign FK/Tweak Layers options as above. limbs.paw makes a fully featured bendy, stretchy paw per the options; it needs four or five connected bones (thigh, shin, paw, optional digit, toe), with Rotation Axis, Limb Segments, B-Bone Segments, and Custom IK Pivot as above.

limbs.paw also offers Assign FK Layers and Assign Tweak Layers to place the FK chain and tweak controls in their own bone collections apart from the IK bones. limbs.front_paw derives from limbs.paw with extended IK suited to front paws: the extra IK caps how much the angle between the shin and paw bones (2nd and 3rd) can change as the main IK control moves and rotates, and works best when the shin isn't parallel to the thigh or paw at rest (some bend in every paw joint). Its Heel IK Influence sets the extended IK's strength — at full, rotating the main IK control or digit bone won't affect the paw bone's rotation, while lower values blend. limbs.rear_paw derives from limbs.paw with extended IK suited to rear paws: the extra IK keeps the thigh and paw bones (1st and 3rd) close to parallel as the main IK control moves, and works best when those bones start nearly parallel at rest. limbs.super_palm builds a palm system from the distance between palm bones; it needs at least two bones childed to one parent, with the property set on the inner palm bone (think the index metacarpus) and the control appearing on the last palm bone (think the pinky metacarpus). Both Sides generates controls on both palm sides with blended influence on the inner bones, and Primary Rotation Axis (X, Z) sets the automatic rotation axis for the palm bones. limbs.spline_tentacle implements a flexible tentacle whose IK uses the Spline IK constraint: the control bones define a Bezier curve's control points and the bone chain follows the curve. Those control points fall into start, middle, and end groups — the middle controls stay visible and active, while start and end types can be shown or hidden dynamically via properties and, when enabled, appear beside their permanent start/end control to be moved from there. Its options: Extra Start Controls and Extra End Controls (how many optional start/end controls to generate); Middle Controls (how many middle controls); and Tip Control, which sets how the curve stretches and how the final bone behaves — Stretch To Fit (the whole chain stretches to the curve's length, with an end twist control for chain twist), Direct Tip Control (an IK end control directly drives the chain's last bone like a limb's IK while also driving the curve's end, with the middle bones stretching to bridge the gap; twist up to 180° is deduced from the end control's orientation, and larger amounts need an End Twist Estimate slider to avoid flipping), and Manual Squash & Stretch (full manual control over chain scaling, the chain covering as much of the curve as its length allows, where the start control manages uniform squash/stretch and the end twist control manages both chain twist and tip scale, blended along the length). Also: Radius Scaling (scale the controls to vary chain thickness along the curve), Maximum Radius (the cap for that scaling), FK Controls (generate an FK chain plus IK-FK snapping), and Assign FK Layers.

## Rotation Modes

Blender offers several ways to express rotations, each with trade-offs and suited to different cases — there's no single best mode. Across all of them, positive angles turn counter-clockwise and negative angles clockwise. Although you can rotate using the global or local transform orientations, those axes don't work for defining rotations, because each axis's effect can't be isolated from the other two: apply three given X, Y, Z values via global or local axes and the final orientation changes with the order you apply them, so proper rotation coordinate systems are needed. Euler modes use a so-called Euler gimbal — three axes in a hierarchy where one is on top with a child axis below it, that child in turn parenting the bottom axis. Which axis is top, middle, and bottom depends on the gimbal, and there are six combinations (XYZ, XZY, YXZ, YZX, ZXY, ZYX), each named by listing the axes from the bottom of the hierarchy up to the top. The trouble starts when the axes lose their mutual perpendicularity: rotating the middle axis drags the bottom one with it, and as the bottom axis nears 90° (or an equivalent angle) it aligns with the top axis, costing you a rotation axis — "gimbal lock" — which can make animated interpolation discontinuous. You can see the gimbal's configuration in the 3D Viewport by enabling the Rotate object gizmo, setting it to Gimbal from the header, and putting the active object into an Euler mode: rotating around the middle axis (the Y axis in XYZ mode) quickly leaves you with only two axes, and in locked XYZ a rotation around X then matches one around Z, so X rotations effectively vanish. The upside of Euler is that animation curves are easy to read and edit, but take care as the middle axis approaches 90°. Axis Angle mode defines an axis (X, Y, Z) and a rotation angle (W) about it: with interactive rotation the X, Y, Z stay within 1.0 in absolute value and W stays between 0 and 180 degrees, and to set rotations beyond 180° (multiple revolutions) you must edit W directly, though the next interactive rotation readjusts it (likewise for axis values). It suits elements revolving around a fixed axis or animating one component (axis or angle) at a time, while animating both at once can give unexpected results; the Gimbal gizmo here shows three orthogonal axes with Z along the defined rotation axis (pointing toward the (X, Y, Z) point). Axis-angle is free of gimbal lock, but its curves are quite unintuitive and hard to edit when you animate axis and angle together. Quaternion mode also uses four values (X, Y, Z, W) where X, Y, Z give an axis and W an angle, but quite differently from axis-angle, with the relationship among all four being what matters: intuitively, the X coordinate rotates the element around X by up to 180°, likewise Y and Z, while W counteracts those rotations toward zero, and the final orientation combines all four effects. Because that relationship sets the orientation, multiplying or dividing all four by a constant yields the same rotation. It's ideal for interpolating between any two orientations, free of gimbal lock and interpolation artifacts; the one drawback is you can't interpolate between orientations more than 180° apart (the animation takes the shortest path), so revolving an element needs many intermediate keyframes at most 180° apart. Its Gimbal gizmo matches the Local one and carries no special meaning.

Quaternion animation curves are also unintuitive and hard to read or edit. For the curious: quaternions are a number system extending the complex numbers, a four-component vector whose components Blender calls X, Y, Z, and W. Rotating interactively in quaternion mode keeps the quaternion's norm (length) constant, and by definition a normalized quaternion's norm is 1.0, so when you select quaternion mode the XYZW components describe a normalized quaternion. Mathematically the norm of a quaternion q is \(\lvert q \rvert = \sqrt{X^2 + Y^2 + Z^2 + W^2}\). However, if you lock one component during interactive transformation, the norm won't stay fixed, since the locked component can't adjust to keep it at unity. Interactive gizmo rotations don't change the norm, but editing a single XYZW component individually can; to restore the norm to 1.0, switch to any rotation mode and back to quaternion. The quaternion components relate closely to axis-angle: working with the normalized quaternion (divide each component by the norm, which doesn't change the orientation), X, Y, Z mean exactly what they do in axis-angle (defining the rotation axis), and W recovers the actual rotation — for a normalized quaternion, \(W = \cos(\frac{a}{2})\), where a is the angle, so \(a = 2 \arccos{W}\). Other considerations: in axis-angle and quaternion modes you can lock rotations per component (rather than by axis) during interactive transforms using the lock buttons next to the Rotation transform buttons. For rotation animation, every keyframe must use the same rotation mode, which must be the object's selected mode throughout the whole animation.

## Extensions Command Line Arguments

The `blender --command extension` command manages Blender extensions; -h / --help shows its help. Its subcommands group into package management — list (list all packages), sync (synchronize with remote repositories), update (upgrade outdated packages), install (install packages), install-file (install from a file), and remove — repository management — repo-list, repo-add, and repo-remove — and extension creation — build (build a package), validate (validate a package), and server-generate (create a listing from all packages). Package management subcommands: `extension list [-h] [-s]` lists packages from every enabled repository, with -s / --sync syncing the remote directory first. `extension sync [-h]` downloads package information for the remote repositories. `extension update [-h] [-s]` downloads and updates outdated packages, -s / --sync syncing first. `extension install [-h] [-s] [-e] [--no-prefs] PACKAGES` installs the named packages (PACKAGES separated by commas, no spaces), where -s / --sync syncs first, -e / --enable enables the extension afterward, and --no-prefs treats the user preferences as read-only so operations that would otherwise edit them (removing extensions or repositories, for instance) don't. `extension install-file [-h] -r REPO [-e] [--no-prefs] FILE` installs a package file into a user repository, with FILE the package file, -r / --repo the repository identifier, and -e and --no-prefs as above. `extension remove [-h] [--no-prefs] PACKAGES` disables and removes the named packages, with --no-prefs as above. Repository management subcommands: `extension repo-list [-h]` lists the repositories in Blender's preferences. `extension repo-add [-h] [--name NAME] [--directory DIRECTORY] [--url URL] [--access-token ACCESS_TOKEN] [--source SOURCE] [--cache BOOLEAN] [--clear-all] [--no-prefs] ID` adds a local or remote repository identified by ID, where --name is the display name (optional), --directory is where it stores local files (optional; a user-directory folder is chosen if omitted), --url is the remote URL (optional; without it the repository is "local," not tied to an external one, with packages installed by file or managed by hand), --access-token supplies a token for remotes that require one, --source is the source type ('USER' or 'SYSTEM', where system repositories are managed outside Blender and are read-only), --cache toggles the package cache (default 1), --clear-all clears all repositories first to simplify test setup, and --no-prefs is as above. `extension repo-remove [-h] [--no-prefs] ID` removes the repository with that ID, --no-prefs as above. Extension creation subcommands: `extension build [-h] [--source-dir SOURCE_DIR] [--output-dir OUTPUT_DIR] [--output-filepath OUTPUT_FILEPATH] [--valid-tags VALID_TAGS_JSON] [--split-platforms] [--verbose]` builds a package in the current directory, where --source-dir is the source directory holding a blender_manifest.toml (defaulting to the current directory), --output-dir is the output directory (current directory by default), --output-filepath is the output file (it should end in .zip; defaults to {id}-{version}.zip from the manifest), --valid-tags points at a file of valid tag lists (a custom .json may be used whose contents are a dictionary of lists keyed by extension type, e.g. {"add-ons": ["Example", "Another"], "theme": ["Other", "Tags"]}, and passing an empty path --valid-tags="" disables tag validation), --split-platforms builds a separate package per platform (adding the platform as a filename suffix before the extension, which helps shrink uploads for packages bundling large platform-specific *.whl modules), and --verbose adds verbose output. `extension validate [-h] [--valid-tags VALID_TAGS_JSON] [SOURCE_PATH]` validates the package metadata in the current directory, where `SOURCE_PATH` is the package source path (a directory of package files or the archive) that must contain a blender_manifest.toml (defaulting to the current directory), and --valid-tags works as for build.

The `extension validate` tags file follows the same form, e.g. {"add-ons": ["Example", "Another"], "theme": ["Other", "Tags"]}, with --valid-tags="" disabling tag validation. `extension server-generate [-h] --repo-dir REPO_DIR [--repo-config REPO_CONFIG] [--html] [--html-template HTML_TEMPLATE_FILE]` generates a listing of every package in a directory so they can be hosted with static files alone: --repo-dir is the remote repository directory; --repo-config is an optional server configuration for information that can't be auto-detected (defaulting to blender_repo.toml in the repository directory), which can, for example, define blocked extensions:
```text
schema_version = "1.0.0"

[[blocklist]]
id = "my_example_package"
reason = "Explanation for why this extension was blocked"
[[blocklist]]
id = "other_extenison"
reason = "Another reason for why this is blocked"
```
--html also creates an index.html alongside the repository JSON for browsing extensions online with static hosting, and --html-template supplies your own HTML file to override the default, where ${body} is replaced with the extensions' contents and ${date} with the creation date.

## Keymap Customization

Keymap customization. Available keys: when customizing keymaps, pick keys that won't collide with Blender's defaults. Ones that are unused and unlikely to be used: the F-keys F5-F8 (including modifier combinations, intentionally left free for user bindings); OSKey (the Windows-Key, Cmd, or Super), which Blender binds to nothing — except on macOS, where Cmd replaces Ctrl unless that would clash with a system binding; and Hyper, a modifier on Wayland and X11 that Blender doesn't bind (it usually needs configuring first). You can also bind modifier keys as primary keys, and to avoid clashing with their normal use, bind them to a double click. Multi-action keys: a single key can do several things by using a Click event instead of Press, with Drag bound to a separate action — handy for mixing a drag action with a press one (for example, toggle a setting with Tab and drag to open a pie menu of its options); the default 3D-Viewport keymap uses this, where Alt-MMB dragging in different directions rotates the view. Common operations: key bindings for pop-ups — menus and panels can take shortcuts even when they're only reachable from submenus: Open a Pop-up Menu (wm.call_menu), Open a Pie Menu (wm.call_menu_pie), and Open a Panel (wm.call_panel, a pop-over). To find a menu's or panel's identifier, enable Interface ‣ Display ‣ Python Tooltips, then hover over the button or item (for submenus, use the back arrow so the submenu doesn't open and steal focus). Key bindings for properties: rather than defining an operator per property, generic operators beginning with wm.context_ adjust properties — among them wm.context_toggle (toggle a Boolean), wm.context_cycle_enum (cycle an enum forward or back), wm.context_menu_enum (pop-up menu for an enum), wm.context_pie_enum (pie menu for an enum), wm.context_scale_float (scale a number, e.g. brush size), wm.context_toggle_enum (toggle between two enum options), and wm.context_modal_mouse (change a value interactively by moving the cursor); see bpy.ops.wm for the full list. Each takes a data_path setting naming the property to change, and finding the data_path needs basic Python — for example, use the Python Console to reach a Boolean property like `bpy.context.object.show_name`, then bind it by adding a keymap item with wm.context_toggle and data_path set to object.show_name (the bpy.context prefix is implicit). See bpy.context for other context attributes, and use the API docs or the Python Console's auto-complete to discover properties.

## Bone Roll

The bone roll is the part of a bone's rest pose that sets its default rotation about its own length, edited in Edit Mode. Recalculate Roll (Edit Mode; Armature ‣ Bone Roll ‣ Recalculate Roll; Shift-N) aligns the roll of all selected bones to a reference: Local Tangent (X, Z, -X, -Z) aligns relative to the axis defined by each bone and its parent — if a bone has no parent it uses the first child instead (even an unconnected one), and for a bone with neither parent nor children it does nothing; Global Axis (X, Y, Z, -X, -Y, -Z) points each bone's Z axis toward the chosen global axis; Active Bone points their Z axes where the active bone's Z currently points; View Axis points their Z axes along the viewport's forward/backward axis (toward the viewer); and Cursor points them at the 3D cursor. Flip Axis shifts the result by 180°, and Shortest Rotation flips by 180° only when needed to keep the absolute roll under 90° (so an initial 160° becomes -20°). Set Roll (Edit Mode; Armature ‣ Bone Roll ‣ Set Roll; Ctrl-R) tweaks the roll of all selected bones, and Clear Roll (Edit Mode; Armature ‣ Bone Roll ‣ Clear Roll; Alt-R) sets it to 0°.

## Delete

Delete Bones (Edit Mode; Armature ‣ Delete ‣ Bones; X) removes the selected bones (selected joints are ignored); deleting a bone mid-chain re-parents its children to its own parent, but unconnected, so the rest of the armature isn't deformed — for instance, a Bone.002 that had been connected to a deleted Bone.001 ends up parented to (but not connected to) Bone. Dissolve (Edit Mode; Armature ‣ Delete ‣ Dissolve; Ctrl-X) is also available.

## Duplicate

Duplicate (Edit Mode; Armature ‣ Duplicate; Shift-D) works on selected bones (selected joints are ignored): as in mesh editing, Shift-D copies them, leaves the copies selected in move mode so you can place them, and preserves the chain when you duplicate part of one (the copied bones keep the same interconnections). A duplicate of a parented bone is parented to the same bone even if that root wasn't part of the selection — but if a bone was parented and connected to an unselected bone, its copy is parented but not connected to that unselected bone (so a duplicated Bone.006 is parented but not connected to Bone.001, shown by a black dashed line, and likewise Bone.007 to Bone.003).

## Extrude

Extrude (Edit Mode; Armature ‣ Extrude; E, Shift-E): pressing E creates a new bone from each selected tip (explicit or implicit), made a child of and connected to that tip's owner, with only the new tips left selected in move mode for placement. You can also do rotating or scaling extrusions (E R, E S) and axis-locked extrusion, as with meshes. Shift-E is a "mirror extruding" tool that by default behaves like the normal extrude, but with X-Axis Mirror editing enabled, each extruded tip yields two new bones sharing a name except for a "_L"/"_R" suffix (left/right per the naming conventions): the "_L" bone behaves like a normally extruded one (move/rotate/scale freely) and the "_R" bone is its mirror across the armature's local X axis. Canceling an extrude snaps the new bones back to their source, making zero-length bones — these are removed on leaving Edit Mode but can confuse, so undo the extrude right away if you notice; you can't just press X as in mesh editing, because the extrude leaves the new tips selected and Delete ignores joints, so removing them without undo means moving the tips, then selecting and deleting the bones. Mouse clicks (Edit Mode; Ctrl-RMB): with at least one bone selected, Ctrl-RMB-clicking adds a new bone whose tip becomes the active element and appears where you clicked, on the view-parallel plane through the 3D cursor (as in mesh editing); the new bone's root placement and parenting depend on the active element — if it's a bone, the root goes on that bone's tip and the new bone is parented and connected to it; if it's a tip, the root goes on that tip and the new bone is parented and connected to the bone owning it; if it's a disconnected root, the root goes there and the new bone is neither parented nor connected to anything; and if it's a connected root, the root goes there and the new bone becomes a parented, connected child of the parent of whichever bone owns that root (which makes sense, since a connected root is also the parent bone's tip — the same as the second case). Because the new tip becomes active, you can repeat Ctrl-RMB clicks to add bones to the end of the same chain.
